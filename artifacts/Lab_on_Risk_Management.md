## 2. Theory

### What is Risk Management?

**Risk Management** in software engineering is a systematic and disciplined approach to identifying, analyzing, planning for, tracking, and controlling events or conditions that threaten the successful delivery of a software project. A "risk" is any potential problem — an uncertain event or condition — that, if it occurs, could have a negative effect on one or more project objectives such as scope, schedule, cost, quality, or stakeholder satisfaction. Risk management does not eliminate uncertainty entirely; rather, it provides a structured framework for anticipating problems before they materialize and for preparing mitigation strategies that minimize their damage.

Risk management is a proactive activity — it is fundamentally different from reactive crisis management. While crisis management deals with problems after they have already occurred and caused damage, risk management seeks to foresee those problems during the planning and development phases, when the cost of addressing them is significantly lower and the range of available responses is significantly wider.

### Procedure of Risk Management

The risk management procedure follows a well-defined sequence of activities, often described as a cyclical process that is repeated throughout the software development lifecycle:

**Step 1 — Risk Identification:**
The first and most critical step is to systematically discover all potential risks that could affect the project. This involves brainstorming sessions, reviewing historical data from similar projects, examining the project's technical architecture, analyzing stakeholder requirements, evaluating the development team's experience, and assessing external dependencies. Risks are typically categorized into types such as:
- **Technical Risks** — relating to the technology stack, platform limitations, performance constraints, database reliability, or algorithm correctness.
- **Project Management Risks** — relating to schedule slippage, resource availability, scope creep, or requirement volatility.
- **Business Risks** — relating to market viability, stakeholder approval, regulatory compliance, or competitive pressure.
- **Security Risks** — relating to data breaches, unauthorized access, cryptographic weaknesses, or privacy violations.
- **Operational/Deployment Risks** — relating to deployment failures, environment configuration, device compatibility, or infrastructure reliability.

**Step 2 — Risk Analysis:**
Once risks are identified, each risk is analyzed to determine two key attributes:
- **Probability** — the likelihood that the risk will actually occur (rated as Very Low, Low, Moderate, High, or Very High).
- **Impact** — the severity of consequences if the risk does occur (rated as Negligible, Marginal, Critical, or Catastrophic).

The combination of probability and impact produces a **Risk Exposure** value that helps prioritize which risks demand immediate attention.

**Step 3 — Risk Prioritization:**
Risks are ranked by their risk exposure (probability × impact). High-exposure risks are addressed first, while low-exposure risks are documented and monitored. A **Risk Table** is the standard tool for organizing this prioritization.

**Step 4 — Risk Mitigation, Monitoring, and Management (RMMM):**
For each significant risk, a RMMM plan is developed containing three components:
- **Mitigation** — proactive steps taken to reduce the probability of the risk occurring or to reduce its impact if it does occur.
- **Monitoring** — indicators, metrics, or checkpoints that signal whether a risk is becoming more or less likely.
- **Management** — contingency plans and corrective actions to execute if the risk materializes despite mitigation efforts.

**Step 5 — Risk Tracking:**
Throughout the project lifecycle, identified risks are periodically revisited to update their probability and impact assessments, evaluate the effectiveness of mitigation strategies, identify new risks that have emerged, and retire risks that are no longer applicable.

## 3. List of Possible Risks of "Custom-Bite Suite"

The following risks have been identified through a comprehensive analysis of the Custom-Bite Suite project's architecture, technology stack, data layer, user experience, deployment strategy, and operational requirements:

1. **R1 — Local-Only SQLite Data Loss:** The application stores all critical data (users, orders, menu, audit logs) exclusively in a local SQLite database (`custom_bite_suite.db`) on the device. If the device is lost, stolen, factory-reset, or the app is uninstalled, all operational data — including order history, customer accounts, financial records, and audit trails — is permanently and irrecoverably lost.

2. **R2 — SHA-256 Password Vulnerability:** User passwords are hashed using SHA-256 via `expo-crypto`. SHA-256 is a fast hashing algorithm and is susceptible to brute-force and rainbow table attacks. Production-grade password hashing algorithms like bcrypt, scrypt, or Argon2 are significantly more resistant due to their computational cost and built-in salting.

3. **R3 — Single-Session Concurrency Conflict:** The application enforces a single-user session model (`app_session` table with `CHECK(id = 1)`). In a real-world restaurant scenario, a manager might need to be logged in simultaneously on multiple devices (e.g., kitchen tablet and office phone), which is architecturally impossible with the current design.

4. **R4 — No Real-Time Push Notifications:** The absence of a backend server means the application cannot send push notifications. A manager will not be alerted when a new order arrives; a customer will not be notified when their order status changes; a rider will not be pinged when a delivery is available. Users must manually navigate to relevant screens to see updated data.

5. **R5 — GPS Location Accuracy and Availability Failure:** The rider tracking and customer location features depend entirely on the device's GPS hardware and the `expo-location` library. In environments with poor GPS signal (indoor locations, dense urban canyons, or bad weather), location data may be inaccurate, delayed, or entirely unavailable, leading to incorrect ETA calculations and failed delivery address pinning.

6. **R6 — Expo SDK and Dependency Breaking Changes:** The application depends on Expo SDK 54, React Native 0.81.5, and over 20 third-party libraries. Major version upgrades to any of these dependencies may introduce breaking API changes, deprecated features, or incompatible native modules that require significant refactoring effort.

7. **R7 — Android Device Fragmentation and Compatibility:** The Android ecosystem includes thousands of device models from dozens of manufacturers, each with different screen sizes, resolutions, hardware capabilities, OS versions (API 21–35+), and vendor-specific UI overlays. The application may exhibit layout inconsistencies, performance degradation, or feature failures on untested device configurations.

8. **R8 — SQLite Concurrent Access Deadlock:** The `expo-sqlite` library has documented limitations with concurrent asynchronous database operations sharing a single connection handle. If multiple rapid state updates trigger simultaneous read/write operations (e.g., a customer placing an order while the manager is advancing another order on the same device), database locking or corruption could occur.

9. **R9 — Unencrypted Local Database:** The SQLite database file (`custom_bite_suite.db`) is stored unencrypted on the device's filesystem. An attacker with physical access to the device (or root access) can extract and read the entire database, exposing user credentials (SHA-256 hashes), personal information (names, emails, phones, addresses), order history, and financial data.

10. **R10 — Fixed Delivery Fee Economic Imbalance:** The delivery fee is hardcoded at $3.50 (`DELIVERY_FEE = 3.5` in `orderMath.ts`) regardless of delivery distance, order size, or demand. For long-distance deliveries, this fee may not cover operational costs; for very short deliveries, it may deter customers from placing small orders.

11. **R11 — Image Storage and Loading Failure:** Dish and banner images can be loaded from external URLs or local device URIs. External URLs may become unreachable (server downtime, domain expiration), and local URIs may become invalid after app data clearing or device migration. Failed image loads result in broken placeholders degrading the visual quality and premium feel of the application.

12. **R12 — Lack of Input Sanitization for SQL Injection:** While the application uses parameterized queries (preventing traditional SQL injection), the audit log `detail` field and free-text fields (delivery notes, special instructions, review comments) store user-provided strings directly. Malicious or malformed input could cause display issues or, if future features render this content in web views, could enable stored cross-site scripting (XSS).

13. **R13 — Order Status Race Condition in Rider Claiming:** When multiple riders attempt to claim the same delivery simultaneously, the optimistic locking mechanism (`WHERE rider_id IS NULL OR rider_id = ?`) relies on SQLite's single-writer transaction model. On a device with the local-only architecture, this is safe, but if the system were ever migrated to a multi-device setup with a shared database, race conditions could result in double-claimed orders.

14. **R14 — No Rate Limiting on Authentication Attempts:** The login system does not implement rate limiting, account lockout, or CAPTCHA mechanisms. An attacker with access to the device can attempt unlimited password guesses against the local database, potentially brute-forcing weak passwords.

15. **R15 — Schema Migration Data Corruption Risk:** The database initialization code in `repository.ts` includes complex migration logic that rebuilds tables (e.g., `_old_ingredients` rename pattern), adds columns via `ALTER TABLE`, and backfills data. If a migration is interrupted (device battery dies, app force-closed) mid-transaction, the database schema could be left in an inconsistent state, potentially causing application crashes on subsequent launches.

16. **R16 — Cleartext HTTP Traffic Exposure:** The Android configuration explicitly allows cleartext (HTTP) traffic (`usesCleartextTraffic: true`), which means image URLs and any other network requests can be transmitted without encryption. This exposes data to man-in-the-middle (MITM) attacks on untrusted networks.

17. **R17 — Cart Data Volatility:** The shopping cart is stored entirely in React component state (in-memory). If the application crashes, is force-closed, the device restarts, or the user navigates away and the component unmounts, all cart contents are lost without warning. There is no persistence layer for cart data.

18. **R18 — No Automated Testing Coverage for Critical Paths:** While the project includes Jest and testing library dependencies, there is no evidence of comprehensive test suites covering critical business logic paths (order placement transactions, status transition validation, refund flow, authentication edge cases). Untested code paths may harbor latent bugs that manifest in production.

19. **R19 — Haversine Formula ETA Inaccuracy:** The ETA calculation uses the Haversine formula (straight-line distance) with a fixed average speed of 30 km/h and a 2-minute buffer. This does not account for actual road networks, traffic conditions, one-way streets, or physical barriers (rivers, highways), leading to potentially misleading ETA estimates for customers.

20. **R20 — Single Point of Failure in AppContext:** The entire application state is managed through a single `AppContext` provider (298 lines with 30+ action methods). If any action method throws an unhandled exception, it can crash the entire application rather than just the affected feature. There is no error boundary isolating individual features.

## 5. Conclusion

This lab provided a clear and practical understanding of **risk management in software engineering** and demonstrated how to systematically **identify, analyze, and apply risk management strategies** for a real-life project. By conducting a thorough risk assessment of the **Custom-Bite Suite** — a single-vendor, multi-role food delivery management platform built with React Native, Expo, and SQLite — we identified **20 distinct risks** spanning five major categories: **Technical** (12 risks), **Security** (4 risks), **Project Management** (2 risks), **Business** (1 risk), and cross-cutting concerns.

The risk analysis revealed that the project's **local-only SQLite architecture** is both its greatest strength (enabling fully offline operation without server infrastructure) and its greatest vulnerability (creating a single point of failure for all data persistence). The most critical risks — **data loss due to device failure (R1)**, **unencrypted database exposure (R9)**, and **schema migration corruption (R15)** — all stem from this architectural decision and carry catastrophic impact ratings.

The exercise also highlighted that several high-probability risks — **lack of push notifications (R4)**, **GPS reliability issues (R5)**, **cart data volatility (R17)**, and **ETA inaccuracy (R19)** — are inherent trade-offs of the current technology choices and can be significantly mitigated through targeted engineering improvements such as background polling, fallback location strategies, state persistence, and routing API integration.

For each identified risk, a comprehensive **RMMM (Risk Mitigation, Monitoring, and Management)** plan was developed, providing actionable mitigation strategies to reduce risk probability, specific monitoring indicators to detect risk emergence, and concrete contingency plans to execute if risks materialize. This structured approach ensures that the development team can proactively manage uncertainty rather than reactively responding to crises, ultimately improving the reliability, security, and operational resilience of the Custom-Bite Suite application.
